The word algorithm comes from the name of a 9th-century Persian mathematician — Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi — whose treatises introduced systematic, step-by-step problem-solving to the Western world. His name, Latinised as Algoritmi, became the word we use today for any well-defined computational procedure.

But the practice of algorithms is far older. Babylonian scribes were recording division algorithms on clay tablets two thousand years before Euclid. Egyptian priests encoded multiplication procedures in papyrus scrolls. The history of algorithms is the history of humanity learning to abstract, generalise, and mechanise thought itself.

For programmers, this history is not academic. The sorting algorithms you use daily were invented by people solving real problems under real constraints. The Big O notation on your code review comes from decades of mathematical struggle to formalise what "efficient" means. The neural networks reshaping the world are direct descendants of ideas Turing had in 1950. Knowing this lineage makes you a better, more grounded programmer.

What Is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is a finite, unambiguous sequence of instructions that transforms an input into a correct output and is guaranteed to terminate. Four properties are required: finiteness (it ends), definiteness (each step is clear), input (zero or more values provided), and output (at least one result produced). This definition, formalised by Donald Knuth in The Art of Computer Programming, is the one every computer scientist uses today.

⏳ The Complete Timeline

Every major algorithmic breakthrough, in order

~1800 BC
Babylonian Division Algorithm
Cuneiform tablets from ancient Babylon record procedures for computing reciprocals using iterative methods — making them arguably the earliest written algorithms. Scribes used sexagesimal (base-60) arithmetic, a system whose legacy survives in our 60-second minutes and 360-degree circles. The tablets demonstrate not just arithmetic results but the procedure for arriving at them — the defining quality of an algorithm.
~1650 BC
Rhind Papyrus — Egyptian Multiplication
The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus, copied by the scribe Ahmes, documents Egyptian multiplication via doubling — a method equivalent to binary multiplication. To multiply 13 × 17, repeatedly double one number, then sum the doublings that correspond to the binary representation of the other. This is structurally identical to how computers multiply integers today. The papyrus also contains early algorithms for distributing bread and beer rations — algorithmic thinking in service of real bureaucratic needs.
~300 BC
Euclid's Algorithm — the Oldest Algorithm Still in Use
Euclid's Elements (Book VII) describes the procedure for finding the Greatest Common Divisor of two integers by repeated subtraction. Donald Knuth calls it "the granddaddy of all algorithms." Its modulo variant is what Python's math.gcd() runs today. More than 2,300 years after it was written, not a single line of the logic has changed. This makes it the oldest algorithm in continuous practical use in human history.
~240 BC
Sieve of Eratosthenes — First Systematic Search Algorithm
Eratosthenes of Cyrene devised a systematic method for finding all prime numbers up to a given limit. The algorithm works by iteratively marking the multiples of each prime, starting from 2. What makes it historically significant is that it's the first documented example of an algorithm designed around the concept of systematic elimination — a pattern that appears in everything from database indexing to machine learning feature selection. With modern optimisations, the same core idea can generate primes in the billions in seconds.
830 AD
Al-Khwarizmi — The Word "Algorithm" is Born
Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi, working in the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, wrote Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr waʾl-muqābala — the word al-jabr giving us "algebra." His separate treatise on Hindu-Arabic numerals, when translated into Latin, was titled Algoritmi de numero Indorum — "Al-Khwarizmi on the Indian numbers." The Latinisation of his name, Algoritmi, became the word "algorithm." He gave the world both systematic algebraic manipulation and the very vocabulary of computational procedure.
1614
Napier's Logarithms — Algorithmic Multiplication Transformed
John Napier's logarithms reduced the labour of multiplication and division to addition and subtraction — a genuine algorithmic transformation that saved astronomers and navigators years of computational work. The slide rule, derived from logarithms, was the analogue computer of its era. Napier's approach of transforming a hard problem into an equivalent easier one is a design pattern that recurs throughout computer science: hashing, Fourier transforms, and dimensionality reduction all follow the same fundamental insight.
1843
Ada Lovelace — First Published Algorithm for a Machine
Ada Lovelace's notes on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine, published as a translation of Luigi Menabrea's paper with extensive annotations, contain what is widely regarded as the first algorithm intended for execution by a mechanical computer. Her Note G describes a procedure for computing Bernoulli numbers — complete with a table of operations that functions exactly like modern pseudocode. More presciently, she wrote that the Engine "might act upon other things besides number" if those things could be expressed as symbols — anticipating general-purpose computing by 100 years.
1854
George Boole — The Algebra of Logic
George Boole's An Investigation of the Laws of Thought reduced logical reasoning to algebraic manipulation with binary values — 0 and 1. Boolean algebra provided the mathematical foundation for all digital circuit design and, through Claude Shannon's work in 1937, for every logic gate in every processor ever built. Every if, and, or, and not in your Python programs is a direct descendant of Boole's 1854 insight.
1936
Alan Turing — The Theoretical Foundation of All Computing
Alan Turing's paper "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem" introduced the Turing Machine — an abstract device that could simulate any computable algorithm. Turing proved that some problems are undecidable (the Halting Problem) and in doing so defined the boundary between what computation can and cannot do. The Church-Turing thesis — that any function computable by an algorithm is computable by a Turing Machine — remains the bedrock of theoretical computer science. Every programming language you use is Turing-complete, meaning it can simulate a Turing Machine.
1945
John von Neumann — Stored-Program Architecture & Merge Sort
Von Neumann's 1945 draft report on EDVAC described the stored-program computer — a machine where both instructions and data live in the same memory, and programs can modify themselves. This architecture is what every computer today uses. In the same period, von Neumann invented Merge Sort — a divide-and-conquer algorithm that runs in O(n log n) time and remains the basis of Python's sorted() function through the hybrid TimSort algorithm. One person, two contributions still inside every device on the planet.
1948
Claude Shannon — Information Theory & the Bit
Shannon's "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" defined the bit as the fundamental unit of information and proved that any message could be encoded, transmitted, and decoded with arbitrarily low error rates, given sufficient redundancy. Shannon entropy — the measure of information content — underpins data compression (ZIP, MP3, JPEG), error-correcting codes (in every hard drive and memory card), and the training signals used in machine learning today.
1956
Dijkstra's Algorithm — Shortest Path, Infinite Applications
Edsger Dijkstra invented his shortest-path algorithm in 20 minutes during a break from shopping, without paper, while sitting at a café. The algorithm finds the minimum-cost path through a weighted graph and runs in O(V² ) time (O((V+E) log V) with a priority queue). Today it powers GPS navigation, network routing protocols, airline route optimisation, game pathfinding (A*), and social network analysis. Dijkstra later recalled designing it specifically to be solvable without pencil and paper, to demonstrate the power of elegant thinking.
1959–1962
Dynamic Programming Matures — Bellman & the Principle of Optimality
Richard Bellman formalised dynamic programming — the technique of solving complex optimisation problems by breaking them into overlapping subproblems and storing results to avoid recomputation. Bellman named it "dynamic programming" partly to disguise its mathematical nature from his Department of Defense funders. DP underlies sequence alignment in bioinformatics, compiler optimisation, portfolio allocation in finance, route planning, and the edit distance algorithms used in spell-checkers and DNA analysis.
1965
Cooley-Tukey — The Fast Fourier Transform
The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) reduced the computational complexity of the Discrete Fourier Transform from O(n²) to O(n log n). Gilbert Strang of MIT called it "the most important numerical algorithm of our lifetime." The FFT is what makes real-time audio processing, digital photography, JPEG compression, 5G wireless communication, medical MRI imaging, and radar signal processing computationally feasible. A calculation that would take hours by hand completes in milliseconds with the FFT.
1971
P vs NP — The Greatest Unsolved Problem in Computer Science
Stephen Cook's 1971 paper introduced the concept of NP-completeness — problems whose solutions can be verified quickly but for which no efficient solution algorithm is known. The question "Does P = NP?" — can every problem whose solution is quickly verifiable also be quickly solved? — remains unanswered and is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems. Its resolution would have consequences for cryptography (all current encryption might break), optimisation, and AI. Most computer scientists believe P ≠ NP, but no proof exists.
1986
Backpropagation — Neural Networks Learn
The backpropagation algorithm, popularised by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams in their 1986 Nature paper, provided a practical method for training multi-layer neural networks by computing the gradient of a loss function with respect to every weight in the network. While the idea had earlier precursors (Werbos, 1974), the 1986 paper demonstrated it working on real cognitive tasks and sparked renewed interest in neural networks. Every deep learning model trained today — GPT, DALL-E, AlphaFold — uses backpropagation at its core.
1994
Shor's Algorithm — Quantum Computing Threatens Cryptography
Peter Shor's quantum algorithm for integer factorisation runs in polynomial time on a quantum computer — meaning it could break RSA encryption, which relies on the difficulty of factoring large numbers on classical machines. Though large-scale quantum computers capable of running Shor's algorithm don't yet exist, its theoretical existence has driven the entire field of post-quantum cryptography. The NIST post-quantum standards finalised in 2024 are a direct response to Shor's 1994 result.
1998
PageRank — The Algorithm That Organised the Web
Larry Page and Sergey Brin's PageRank algorithm, developed as their Stanford PhD research and published in 1998, ranked web pages by treating hyperlinks as votes — weighted by the authority of the linking page. The algorithm is a random walk on a directed graph with a damping factor to ensure convergence. It transformed web search from keyword matching to authority-based ranking, creating Google and reshaping how humanity accesses information. The underlying mathematical structure — eigenvector centrality on a stochastic matrix — appears in network analysis, citation graphs, and recommendation systems.
2017
"Attention Is All You Need" — The Transformer Architecture
Vaswani et al.'s paper introduced the Transformer — a neural network architecture based entirely on attention mechanisms, abandoning the recurrent structures that had dominated sequence modelling. The self-attention mechanism allows every token in a sequence to directly attend to every other token, enabling parallelisation that made training on internet-scale data feasible. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, DALL-E, Whisper, AlphaFold 2, and nearly every frontier AI system built since 2020 is a Transformer or Transformer-derived architecture. It may be the most consequential algorithm published in the 21st century.

🏺 The Ancient Era in Depth

~2000 BC to 500 AD — algorithms as tools for civilisation

🔢 Babylonian Arithmetic (~1800 BC)

The Babylonians worked in base 60 and had procedures for computing square roots, cube roots, and reciprocals that amount to iterative approximation algorithms. Their method for computing √2, found on the Yale Babylonian Collection tablet YBC 7289, is equivalent to what we now call the Newton-Raphson method — rediscovered 3,500 years later. They knew that the procedure converged to the correct answer, even without a formal theory of convergence.

💡 The Babylonian method for square roots: start with a guess x, then repeatedly apply x ← (x + n/x) / 2. This is Newton's method applied to f(x) = x² − n, and it converges quadratically. Python's math.sqrt() ultimately descends from this approach.

🌀 The Sieve of Eratosthenes (~240 BC)

The Sieve is a masterpiece of algorithmic thinking: instead of testing each candidate number for primality, it pre-emptively marks all composites. The insight — that it is more efficient to eliminate than to test — recurs constantly in computer science, from hash tables to Bloom filters to database query planning.

def sieve_of_eratosthenes(limit: int) -> list[int]:
    """Return all primes up to limit."""
    is_prime = [True] * (limit + 1)
    is_prime[0] = is_prime[1] = False

    for i in range(2, int(limit**0.5) + 1):
        if is_prime[i]:
            
            is_prime[i*i :: i] = [False] * len(is_prime[i*i :: i])

    return [i for i, prime in enumerate(is_prime) if prime]

print(sieve_of_eratosthenes(50))
💡 Time complexity: O(n log log n) — one of the most efficient algorithms for small primes. Space complexity: O(n). Eratosthenes didn't have Big O notation, but he intuitively understood that starting marking from i² (not 2i) was more efficient.

📐 Euclid's Algorithm in Modern Python

Euclid's original method used repeated subtraction. The modulo variant (Euclidean algorithm with division) is dramatically faster — it reduces the problem size by at least half every two steps, giving O(log min(a,b)) time complexity. This was proven by Gabriel Lamé in 1844, making it the first algorithm to receive a formal computational complexity analysis.

def gcd_subtraction(a, b):
    while a != b:
        if a > b: a -= b
        else: b -= a
    return a

def gcd_modulo(a, b):
    while b:
        a, b = b, a % b
    return a

def gcd_recursive(a, b):
    return a if b == 0 else gcd_recursive(b, a % b)

import math
print(math.gcd(1071, 462))

📊 Algorithm Complexity — Reading the Language of Efficiency

Big O notation: the vocabulary every programmer needs to evaluate and compare algorithms

Big O notation was formalised by the German mathematician Paul Bachmann in 1894 and popularised by Edmund Landau. It describes how the runtime or memory usage of an algorithm grows as the input size grows — ignoring constants and lower-order terms to focus on the fundamental scaling behaviour. It is the universal language for discussing algorithm efficiency.

📈 Why Complexity Matters in Practice

The difference between O(n log n) and O(n²) for sorting 1 million records: ~20 million operations versus 1 trillion operations. On a machine doing 10⁹ operations per second, that's 0.02 seconds versus 1,000 seconds. Choosing the wrong algorithm doesn't just slow your code — it makes it unusable.

import time, random

data = random.sample(range(10_000), 10_000)

def bubble_sort(arr):
    n = len(arr)
    for i in range(n):
        for j in range(n - i - 1):
            if arr[j] > arr[j+1]:
                arr[j], arr[j+1] = arr[j+1], arr[j]
    return arr

start = time.perf_counter()
sorted(data)                  
fast = time.perf_counter() - start

start = time.perf_counter()
bubble_sort(data.copy())      
slow = time.perf_counter() - start

print(f"TimSort:     {fast*1000:.2f} ms")   
print(f"Bubble Sort: {slow*1000:.2f} ms")
💡 Python's sorted() uses TimSort — a hybrid of Merge Sort (von Neumann, 1945) and Insertion Sort, invented by Tim Peters in 2002. It achieves O(n log n) worst-case while being O(n) for already-sorted data. It's also used in Java's Arrays.sort() for objects.

🔍 Searching

🔍 Binary Search — O(log n)

Binary search (formalised in the 1940s–60s, though the principle is ancient) divides a sorted array in half with each comparison, reducing an n-element search to at most log₂(n) steps. Finding one record in a billion takes just 30 comparisons. It is the foundation of database index lookups, dictionary autocomplete, and version control bisection (git bisect).

def binary_search(arr: list, target: int) -> int:
    """Return index of target in sorted arr, or -1 if not found."""
    left, right = 0, len(arr) - 1

    while left <= right:
        mid = (left + right) // 2   
        if   arr[mid] == target: return mid
        elif arr[mid]  < target: left  = mid + 1
        else:                    right = mid - 1
    return -1

data = list(range(0, 1_000_000, 2))   
print(binary_search(data, 999_998))    
print(binary_search(data, 999_999))
🔀 Sorting

🔀 Merge Sort — O(n log n) — von Neumann, 1945

Divide-and-conquer at its most elegant: split the array in half, recursively sort each half, then merge the sorted halves. The merge step is the key insight — two sorted arrays can be merged in O(n) time by comparing front elements. Python's sorted() is based on this principle.

def merge_sort(arr: list) -> list:
    if len(arr) <= 1:
        return arr

    mid   = len(arr) // 2
    left  = merge_sort(arr[:mid])
    right = merge_sort(arr[mid:])

    return _merge(left, right)

def _merge(left: list, right: list) -> list:
    result, i, j = [], 0, 0
    while i < len(left) and j < len(right):
        if left[i] <= right[j]:
            result.append(left[i]); i += 1
        else:
            result.append(right[j]); j += 1
    return result + left[i:] + right[j:]

print(merge_sort([38, 27, 43, 3, 9, 82, 10]))
🗺️ Graph Algorithms

🗺️ Dijkstra's Shortest Path — Edsger Dijkstra, 1956

Given a weighted graph, find the minimum-cost path from a source node to all other nodes. Powers Google Maps, network routing, airline connections, and game AI. The algorithm uses a priority queue (min-heap) to always process the cheapest unvisited node next.

import heapq
from collections import defaultdict

def dijkstra(graph: dict, source: str) -> dict:
    """Return shortest distances from source to all nodes."""
    dist = defaultdict(lambda: float('inf'))
    dist[source] = 0
    heap = [(0, source)]   

    while heap:
        d, u = heapq.heappop(heap)
        if d > dist[u]: continue   
        for v, weight in graph[u]:
            nd = d + weight
            if nd < dist[v]:
                dist[v] = nd
                heapq.heappush(heap, (nd, v))
    return dict(dist)

roads = {
    'A': [('B',4),('C',2)], 'B': [('D',3)],
    'C': [('B',1),('D',5)], 'D': []
}
print(dijkstra(roads, 'A'))
🧠 Dynamic Programming

🧠 Fibonacci — Naive vs DP vs Matrix Exponentiation

The Fibonacci sequence is the classic teaching example for algorithmic optimisation. The naive recursive solution is O(2ⁿ). Memoisation (top-down DP) makes it O(n). Bottom-up DP uses O(1) space. Matrix exponentiation computes the nth Fibonacci in O(log n) time — the same asymptotic improvement that binary search achieves over linear search.

from functools import lru_cache

def fib_naive(n): return n if n < 2 else fib_naive(n-1) + fib_naive(n-2)

@lru_cache(maxsize=None)
def fib_memo(n): return n if n < 2 else fib_memo(n-1) + fib_memo(n-2)

def fib_dp(n):
    a, b = 0, 1
    for _ in range(n): a, b = b, a + b
    return a

print(fib_dp(50))
💡 The @lru_cache decorator is Python's built-in memoisation. It stores previously computed results in a dictionary, turning the exponential tree of recursive calls into a linear sequence of unique computations. This is dynamic programming in one decorator.

🤖 The AI & Machine Learning Era

1986 to present — algorithms that learn from data

🔁 Backpropagation — The Algorithm That Trained a Generation of AI

Backpropagation computes the gradient of a loss function with respect to every weight in a neural network by applying the chain rule of calculus backwards through the network's layers. Each weight is updated in proportion to how much it contributed to the error. Over millions of iterations on millions of examples, the network learns representations that generalise to new data.

import numpy as np

def sigmoid(x):      return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-x))
def sigmoid_deriv(x): return sigmoid(x) * (1 - sigmoid(x))

X = np.array([[0,0],[0,1],[1,0],[1,1]])
y = np.array([[0],[1],[1],[0]])

np.random.seed(42)
W1 = np.random.randn(2, 4) * 0.1   
W2 = np.random.randn(4, 1) * 0.1   

for epoch in range(10_000):
    
    z1 = X @ W1;     a1 = sigmoid(z1)
    z2 = a1 @ W2;    a2 = sigmoid(z2)
    loss = y - a2

    d2 = loss * sigmoid_deriv(z2)
    d1 = (d2 @ W2.T) * sigmoid_deriv(z1)

    W2 += a1.T @ d2 * 0.5   
    W1 += X.T  @ d1 * 0.5

print(np.round(a2, 2))

🎯 The Transformer — Vaswani et al., 2017

The self-attention mechanism at the heart of the Transformer computes, for each token in a sequence, a weighted sum of all other tokens' value vectors — where the weights are determined by how relevant each other token is to the current one. This allows the model to capture long-range dependencies that recurrent networks struggled with, and to process sequences in parallel rather than sequentially.

import numpy as np

def scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V):
    """
    The core operation of every Transformer model.
    Q: queries, K: keys, V: values  — all shape (seq_len, d_k)
    """
    d_k = Q.shape[-1]
    
    scores = Q @ K.T / np.sqrt(d_k)
    
    scores -= scores.max(axis=-1, keepdims=True)  
    weights = np.exp(scores) / np.exp(scores).sum(axis=-1, keepdims=True)
    
    return weights @ V

np.random.seed(0)
seq_len, d_k = 4, 8
Q = np.random.randn(seq_len, d_k)
K = np.random.randn(seq_len, d_k)
V = np.random.randn(seq_len, d_k)

output = scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V)
print(output.shape)
⚡ GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, AlphaFold 2, Whisper, and essentially every frontier AI system built after 2020 uses this exact operation — scaled dot-product attention — as its fundamental computational primitive. Vaswani et al.'s eight-page paper may be the highest-impact computer science paper of the 21st century.

Frequently Asked Questions

About algorithms, their history, and how to study them

What is the oldest algorithm known to humanity?

+

The oldest written algorithms we have strong evidence for are the Babylonian division and reciprocal procedures on clay tablets from approximately 1800–1700 BC. The Rhind Papyrus (~1650 BC) documents Egyptian multiplication. However, Euclid's GCD algorithm (~300 BC) is the oldest algorithm still in continuous practical use — running inside every modern programming language's standard library.

Why is it called an "algorithm"?

+

The word comes from the Latinisation of the name of Persian mathematician Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (~780–850 AD). When his treatise on Hindu-Arabic numerals was translated into Latin in the 12th century, it was titled Algoritmi de numero Indorum — "Al-Khwarizmi on the Indian numbers." The Latinised form of his name, Algoritmi, gradually became the common noun "algorithm" for any systematic, step-by-step procedure.

Did Alan Turing invent the computer?

+

Turing defined the theoretical foundations of computation — what it means for a problem to be computable, and the abstract model (Turing Machine) that all computers are based on. The physical stored-program computer was largely built by von Neumann, Eckert, Mauchly, and others. Turing did work on early physical computers (including ACE at NPL) and built the Bombe machine at Bletchley Park. It's most accurate to say Turing invented the theoretical computer; the physical digital computer was a collaborative achievement across many people and institutions in the 1940s.

What does P vs NP mean and why does it matter?

+

P is the class of problems solvable efficiently (in polynomial time). NP is the class of problems whose solutions can be verified efficiently. The question "P = NP?" asks whether every problem that can be quickly verified can also be quickly solved. If P = NP, current encryption — which relies on factoring being hard — would collapse instantly. Optimisation problems across logistics, scheduling, drug discovery, and AI would become trivially solvable. Most mathematicians believe P ≠ NP, but no proof has been found despite 50+ years of effort. It remains one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems, with a $1,000,000 reward.

What is Big O notation and who invented it?

+

Big O notation describes the upper bound on how an algorithm's runtime grows as input size n grows, ignoring constants and lower-order terms. It was introduced by German mathematician Paul Bachmann in his 1894 book Die Analytische Zahlentheorie and further developed by Edmund Landau — which is why it's sometimes called Bachmann-Landau notation. Donald Knuth popularised it for algorithm analysis in computer science through The Art of Computer Programming. Today it is the universal language for discussing algorithmic efficiency.

How do I get started learning algorithms as a Python programmer?

+

Start with the classics: implement binary search, bubble sort, merge sort, and Euclid's GCD from scratch to build intuition. Then study sorting algorithms comparatively — why is Merge Sort O(n log n) while Bubble Sort is O(n²)? Next, learn graph traversal (BFS and DFS) and then Dijkstra's shortest path. For dynamic programming, start with Fibonacci memoisation, then move to the classic problems: coin change, longest common subsequence, and knapsack. LeetCode, Exercism, and Knuth's TAOCP are the best practice resources. Implement everything in Python — the clarity of the language lets you focus on the algorithm, not the syntax.

What is the most important algorithm of the 21st century so far?

+

A strong case can be made for the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). Its scaled dot-product attention mechanism is the computational core of GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, AlphaFold 2, and Whisper — systems that are reshaping medicine, science, software engineering, and creative work simultaneously. Other strong candidates include the diffusion model algorithm (powering image generation) and the proximal policy optimisation algorithm used in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) to align large language models.
